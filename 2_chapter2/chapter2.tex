\section{Automatic Speech Recognition and Neural Networks}\label{lr_sec:asr_early_dnns}
ASR has historically been performed with Hidden Markov Models (HMMs) \cite{galeshmm2008, Juang01081991}. However, their probabilistic approach required fixed assumptions about speech, which made them hard to adapt to unseen conditions. To model variations in speech such as accent, dialect, language, noise, context and cross-talk, a different approach was required. So in 2012, when Krizhevsky et al. \cite{krizhevsky2012imagenet} published their breakthrough paper on image classification with DNNs, ASR research shifted its focus. In 2014, sequence learning with DNNs changed the field of Natural Language Processing (NLP) \cite{sutskever2014sequence}. Then, in the same year, Graves \& Jaitly showed that a DNN architecture could produce competitive ASR results to HMMs, if trained with CTC Loss. However, their DNN-HMM hybrid model still resulted in the best results \cite{pmlr-v32-graves14}.

At the end of 2014, Hannun et al. \cite{hannun2014deep} submitted their paper detailing an ASR model designed entirely with DNNs. Previous attempts had been made to improve ASR by integrating DNNs with HMMs \cite{hintondnnhmm, Hamidcnnhmm}. However, none had, so far, been successful with an entirely DNN architecture. They called their model Deep Speech and the core of the architecture employed Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) \cite{Graves2012}. RNNs are neural networks built for sequential data, such as text or audio. When learning the current token in a sequence, they carry the hidden states from the previous token forward. So, each new token learns contextual information from prior tokens.

Deep Speech learns from log-mel spectrograms. Mel-spectrograms contain a variable-size filterbank of frequency bins. Mel-filterbanks contain narrow-band bins at low frequencies and high-band bins at high frequencies, mimicking human perception of speech. It was trained using a CTC Loss. They evaluate the ASR performance of their model using Word Error Rate (WER). Deep Speech also uses a 4-gram language model (LM). An LM is a probabilistic model that assigns a likelihood to a sequence of words. An n-gram LM estimates probabilities based on the n previous words.

%\hl{It would be nice to have a figure here, maybe of a spectrogram I have generated from LibriSpeech.}

Deep Speech was trained on large datasets for the time. By using model parallelism \cite{hannun2014deep}, they were able to significantly decrease training time compared to previous attempts. When trained on 2.3k hours of conversational Switchboard \cite{godfreyswitchboard1992} and Fisher (FSH) \cite{cieri2004fisher}, they were able to produce comparable ASR WER to other State of the Art (SOTA) ASR models. They then trained Deep Speech on 5k hours of clean, read speech, collected in-house, and tested on a subset of the same data. They then compared test accuracy of Deep Speech on their in-house dataset to SOTA HMM and HMM hybrid models. They found that it had a lower WER than all SOTA models, even on a noisy subset of the data \cite{hannun2014deep}. These results proved that with more data and efficient training time, DNN architectures could, in fact, outperform traditional HMM models.

In 2015, Chan et al. \cite{chan2015listen} created a DNN ASR architecture that compressed long sequences by using a pyramid structure of layers. Each layer compresses and concatenates previous audio frames before feeding them to the next layer. This allowed the model to learn sequence structure, with increasingly fewer computations at each layer. The layer architectures they use for sequence modelling are  Bidirectional Long Short Term Memory RNNs (BLSTM) \cite{Graves2012}. LSTMs extend RNNs by adding a gated cell state that preserves multiple learned features from earlier tokens in a sequence. At each step, the model uses the current token and previous hidden state to decide which features to retain in memory. BLSTMs then process the sequence in both directions. Each token is represented using hidden state context from both preceding and following tokens.

They also use an attention mechanism in the decoder to learn additional context information. At each step in training, an attention vector is created. The decoder then compares the vector for the current step to previous steps to learn sequence context. This attention mechanism was further adapted for speech recognition by Chorowski et al. \cite{chorowski2015attention}. They introduce a location-aware attention model for end-to-end ASR. Their method incorporates 1D convolution over the previous attention weights to produce learned location features. These are combined with the decoder state and encoder states to predict the next token output.

In 2016, Amodei, and the same team at Baidu, published their work on Deep Speech 2 \cite{amodei2016deep}. Deep Speech 2 experimented with several architecture choices. They first compared an RNN architecture to Gated Recurrent Units (GRU) \cite{cho2014grulearning} and batch normalisation \cite{pmlr-v37-ioffe15}. They found that the use of both techniques lowered WER when compared to the vanilla RNN architecture. However, while GRUs generally use less memory, they are still sequential models and therefore more computationally expensive than vanilla RNNs. Due to their sequential nature, they still produced high error rates on long utterances. 

Finally, they tested how Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) \cite{lecuncnn1998} feature extraction could improve performance. CNNs only learn from a single input frame and are therefore not suited for learning long-range dependencies in sequential data. They are, however, useful for projecting input data into feature space. Their best ASR performance was found when using 3 2D CNNs with mel-spectrogram inputs. In this case, WER was substantially reduced on their test data, especially in noisy conditions.

They trained two models, one trained on 11.9k hours of labelled English data and another model on 9.4k of labelled Mandarin data. The English model produced lower WER than human attempts on the Wall Street Journal (WSJ) \cite{paul1992design} and a new dataset from 2015 LibriSpeech \cite{librispeech}. LibriSpeech contains almost 1k hours of clean, read, American English, annotated speech. Due to the quality and size of the dataset, it would become a common benchmark for many ASR models. While Deep Speech 2 performed well on LibriSpeech, WER was substantially higher when it was tested on the VoxForge accented English speech data. Despite the large size of the training data, Deep Speech 2 struggled to adapt to out-of-domain English accented data. ASR research was now in need of 2 things: efficient, sequence-aware architectures and a solution to expensive data labelling.


\subsection{CTC and WER}\label{lr_subsec:ctc_were}
In this section, we will briefly cover some important concepts to ASR: CTC Loss and Word/Character/Phoneme Error Rate. Both of these concepts were used in early architectures and continue into modern ASR. %They are also used throughout the research covered in this thesis.

\subsubsection{Label Error Rate}
In order to evaluate the transcription produced by an ASR model, the most common metric is the label error rate. To compute the error rate we split both the hypothesis and target transcriptions into labels. These labels are most commonly either words, characters or phonemes. The error rate is calculated by comparing how many labels in the hypothesis align correctly with labels in the target.

\begin{equation}
    ErrorRate=\frac{S+D+I}{N}
\end{equation}

Here we mark the number of labels in the target transcription as $N$. The error rate, therefore, is calculated as a ratio of incorrect labels in the hypothesis to correct labels in the target. Incorrect labels are categorised as either substitutions, $S$, deletions, $D$, or insertions, $I$, (SDI). Substitutions are labels that have been incorrectly substituted for another label. Deletions are labels incorrectly deleted from the target transcription. Insertions are labels incorrectly inserted into the transcription. To calculate the SDIs we use the Levenshtein distance \cite{levenshtein1966binary}, often also called the edit distance. Levenshtein uses dynamic programming to calculate the smallest number of SDI errors necessary to map the hypothesis to the target transcriptions.

When the transcription labels are divided into words, we refer to this as the Word Error Rate (WER). For character labels, it is the Character Error Rate (CER). For phoneme labels, it is the Phoneme Error Rate (PER). 

\subsubsection{Connectionist Temporal Classification}
In 2006, Grave et al. \cite{graves2006connectionist} trained neural networks to align an input sequence to an output sequence of different lengths. They named their method Connectionist Temporal Classification (CTC) and trained an RNN model for an ASR task. Their models were trained with an output classifier layer. This layer has a number of outputs that equals the number of characters in a given alphabet plus 1. The extra output maps to a blank token that does not map to a character token. 

For training, CTC uses maximum likelihood with prefix search. Traditionally, softmax and argax functions would be applied to the output activations to select the most likely character for each frame. Instead of throwing away lower probability characters, CTC retains all classification activations at each frame for each character in the output sequence. These activations are then interpreted as the probability of observing each label in a given frame. 

To calculate the probability of a label occurring in a given frame, the output layer activation for each possible label is weighted by the activations for that label in adjacent frames. They also weight by the activations for labels that are adjacent in the output sequence. The probabilities for each character are then calculated as the sum of these weighted activations. These probabilities are then used to calculate the boundaries of each label in the output sequence. 

Boundaries are used to map multiple input frames to each label in the output sequence. These boundaries are marked in CTC with blank tokens that do not equate to any text characters. For backpropagation in training, the probabilities are differentiated with respect to the activation weights. The CTC loss objective is expressed as minimising the negative sum of the product of the log probabilities for each label in every frame.

\begin{equation}\label{lr_equ:ctc_loss}
\begin{split}
    \mathcal{L}_{CTC}&= -ln(p(Z|X)) \\
                     &=-\sum^U_{u=1} ln(p(z|x))\\
                     &=-\sum^U_{u=1} \prod_{t=1}^T ln(p(x_t)) 
\end{split}
\end{equation}

The negative maximum likelihood is shown in equation \ref{lr_equ:ctc_loss}, where p is the probability function. The output sequence, $Z=(z_0,z_1,...,z_U)$, is the target sequence of length $U$ and the activations at each frame, $X=(x_0,x_1,...,x_T)$, are length $T$. This equation calculates the sum of the products of each $t$ log probability, $ln(p(a|b))$, for each $u$ output token. 

When evaluating a finetuned model during inference, we use "best path" decoding, typically referred to as "greedy" decoding. This involves applying the softmax and argmax functions to the activations collected at each frame to collect the predicted characters. A string of the most likely characters is produced. Since there are more input frames than output characters, duplicates and blanks are likely produced, $Y=(-,h,h,h,-,-,i,i,-)$. First, greedy decoding removes the blanks, $Y=(h,h,h,i,i)$ and then removes duplicate characters, $Y=(h,i)$. This final output is considered the model's prediction of the output sequence.




\subsection{Transformers}

Until 2017, sequential tasks, such as ASR, had typically used RNNs or LSTMs \cite{graves2012rnnt}. These architectures process tokens one at a time and carry hidden states forward to preserve context. However, this approach makes long-sequence training computationally expensive. Architectures based solely on CNN layers were also shown to get respectable results on datasets such as LibriSpeech, with less computation \cite{librispeech, quartznet}. However, they are unable to learn contextual information between frames and so underperformed when compared. In 2017, Vaswani et al. \cite{vaswani2017attention} published their work on a new architecture based on multi-head attention \cite{bahdanau2014neural, luong2015effective}. They created an architecture that can be trained on multiple tokens in a sequence simultaneously at a lower computational cost compared to RNNs. They use a multi-head attention mechanism and named their architecture the transformer.

An attention mechanism maps a query and a set of key-value pairs to an output, where the query, key and value are all vectors. The query and key values are used to calculate a weighting multiplier to each value, and the output is the weighted sum of the values. The initial transformer architecture Vaswani et al. \cite{vaswani2017attention} employed used an encoder-decoder structure. This architecture maps one sequence of symbols to continuous representations. Figure~\ref{lr_fig:transformer_encoder} shows the encoder architecture, with the multi-head attention mechanism and the scaled-dot product expanded. Training the encoder, starts with a data sequence, $X = X_0, X_1,...,X_{N-1}$, such as words in a sentence or audio frames of a spoken utterance. The encoder employs self-attention by using the same input vector to create the query $Q$, key $K$ and value $V$ vectors through a projection layer. 

\begin{equation}
    Q=XW_Q, K=XW_K, V=XW_V
\end{equation}

Here, $W_Q, W_K$ and $W_V$ are learned throughout the course of training. The attention weighting is then calculated by the dot product of query and key vectors. They scale the dot product by the root of the dimension of the key vector $d_k$ and then apply softmax. The weighting is calculated and multiplied by the value vector, reffered to as the caled dot product in Figure~\ref{lr_fig:transformer_encoder}. 

\begin{equation}
    Attention(Q,K,V)=softmax(\frac{QK^T}{\sqrt{d_k}})V
\end{equation}

Instead of performing a single attention function, transformers implement multi-head attention, depicted in Figure~\ref{lr_fig:transformer_encoder}. As seen in equation~\ref{lr_equ:multihead_attention}, multi-head attention concatenates, $Concat()$, $h$ linear projections, $head_h$, for the queries, keys and values. This effectively calculates attention multiple times in parallel, on the same projected token. The model to jointly learns information from different representational space simultaneously. 

Multi-head attention is calculated as:

\begin{equation}
    MultiHead(Q,K,V)=Concat(head_1,...,head_h)W^O
\end{equation}\label{lr_equ:multihead_attention}

\begin{equation}
    head_i=Attention(QW^Q_i,KW^K_i,VW^V_i)
\end{equation}


For the experiments in this paper they use 8 attention heads, $h=8$. The outputs of the multi-head attention mechanism are then concatenated, normalised and passed on to a single feed-forward layer, ReLU activation and a second layer before layer normalisation, which then creates the output to the transformer block. If the model contains multiple transformer self-attention blocks, the output of the initial transformer is fed into the input of the next block. Where the scaled dot product is applied to each linear projection.

    \centering

    \caption{The transformer encoder architecture, with multi-head attention and the scaled dot-product. Adapted from: (Vaswani et al. 2017)}
    \label{lr_fig:transformer_encoder}
\end{figure}

The decoder utilises two attention mechanisms, self-attention and cross-attention. The first self-attention mechanism has the target sequence as an input and follows the same self-attention mechanism of the encoder blocks. After self-attention is applied instead of passing the outputs onto a feed-forward network, they perform cross-attention. In this case, cross-attention takes the query from the decoder input and the key and value from the encoder output. Cross attention is performed with the same mechanisms as self-attention otherwise. The outputs of the cross-attentions are added and normalised with the outputs of the decoder self-attention. This is then fed into a feed-forward network and passed either to the next block or, at the final block, a single linear classification layer.

For both the encoder and the decoder, one final addition is made by adding positional encoding to the input sequences. A vector of sine or cosine values is added to the output of the projection layer. This addition gives each embedding for a token a clear, relative position within the vector. 


Vaswani et al. \cite{vaswani2017attention} test their architecture by training it for a translation task between multiple languages. They train for two tasks: English to German text translation and English to French text translation. In each case, English is the input, and either German or French is the target. They find that for both targets, the transformer architecture results in better BLEU scores \cite{papineni2002bleu} than all previous SOTA models. They also find that transformers achieve this improved performance with substantially fewer Floating Point Operations (FLOPs) when compared to previous models.

They experiment with different optimal architecture types and find that increasing the number of attention heads beyond a certain point had diminishing returns. They also find that when using learned positional encoding, the results were also identical to adding absolute sinusoid positions. \subsection{Tranformers in Speech}\label{lr_subsec:transformer_in_speech}
Due to their ability to process sequences in parallel, transformers were used quickly implemented into SOTA ASR architectures. In 2019, Dong et al. \cite{dong2018speech} used the encoder-decoder architecture to produce lower WER on WSJ when compared to previous architectures. Attention mechanisms were also used in speech by placing them before CNN layers \cite{bello2019attention, gulati2020conformer}. A common architecture emerged in ASR that included a CNN feature extractor, with masked \cite{park2019specaugment} mel-spectrograms as the input and a transformer or conformer encoder. 

In 2022, this architecture was used to train the multilingual ASR model Whisper \cite{whisper}. Whisper inputs a masked mel-spectrogram to a CNN feature extractor, which feeds into the transformer encoder-decoder architecture using conformer blocks \cite{gulati2020conformer} and trained with cross-entropy loss. Conformers are transformer blocks with a convolutional layer placed before the feed-forward output layer. Whisper increased the scale of its model over many previous architectures, with its largest incarnation having over 1.5B parameters. They produced multiple models of different sizes, with the smallest having 39M parameters. They trained the large model on 680k hours of data, including 99 languages. All of the language data was transcribed, and all transcriptions were normalised to standard vocabularies for each language. 

Whisper is able to transcribe at least 99 languages out of the box and can be trained to recognise more. Whisper boasted lower WER on datasets such as Librispeech \cite{librispeech}, MLS \cite{MLS} and VoxPopuli \cite{wang-etal-2021-voxpopuli}. However, since Whisper is trained with transcribed data, the model can be quite restrictive. Adapting the model to new data in a pretrained language can be difficult if Out Of Vocabulary (OOV) words or characters are necessary. Whisper can be used without further training for pretrained languages. However, for low-resource languages, results are not always in line with SOTA  \cite{FLEURS_XLSR_WHISPER}. Training the largest Whisper model can also be computationally expensive, with its very high parameter count. They also note that the WER recorded for small Whisper models is much higher than the largest versions of Whisper.

Adaptability is a common problem with ASR models due to the diversity of vocabularies in transcribed data, even within the same language. Normalising for every OOV label between datasets is not always possible. Success may depend on the context of the new speech data and the vocabulary a model was trained on. Domain-specific words, such as terms used in certain professions, may not be included in a large dataset of book readings such as LibriSpeech or MLS \cite{librispeech, MLS, luo2021cross}. The ideal solution to this problem would be to train a model on a large set of data without the need for transcriptions. 

In 2019, Schneider et al. \cite{schneider2019wav2vec} showed that ASR could be accomplished effectively by training on unlabelled speech data. With their model wav2vec, they use a contrastive loss function to predict future speech frames. The model is fully convolutional, using only CNNs in the architecture. They train an encoder to distil frames of audio down to speech representations and a context network to learn contextualised representations. The predictive training does not require labelled data for the model to learn. We refer to training methods that do not require labelled data as Self-Supervised Learning (SSL). The wav2vec model required two stages of training, the first SSL stage, which we refer to as pretraining, and a fine-tuning stage on labelled data. While wav2vec was innovative, the WER it produced was not competitive with SOTA results at the time.


In 2020, Baevski et al. \cite{wav2vec} expanded on the concepts introduced to ASR with wav2vec by creating another SSL ASR model, wav2vec 2.0. Wav2vec 2.0 would introduce a transformer architecture for the context network. It would still be trained on contrastive loss, but this time by contrasting context network outputs with learned, quantized, representations. This new approach to ASR would beat previous SOTA WER on LibriSpeech while requiring only small speech datasets for fine-tuning. Wav2vec 2.0 quickly became a widely cited benchmark for ASR models and is the model used throughout this thesis. We explore the architecture of wav2vec 2.0 in more detail in Section \ref{lr_sec:wav2vec2}.

Other SSL approaches have been taken, most notably HuBERT \cite{hsu2021hubert}. HuBERT takes inspiration from the SSL NLP model BeRT \cite{devlin-etal-2019-bert}. BeRT trained transformers on masked text predictions. To train HuBERT on speech, the input to the model uses a CNN feature extractor on a raw waveform, the features are randomly masked and fed to the transformer blocks. The output of the transformer blocks are then compared to multiple k-means clustering models trained on mel-spectrograms from the input speech. The outputs of the clustering models and the final transformer block are then quantised into the same latent space. They are compared for similarity to create the final loss value. With these methods, they achieve WER relative to wav2vec 2.0 on LibriSpeech \cite{librispeech}. 

Large, transformer based, archietctures had shown that they could achieve low error rates while doing the bulk of learning during pretraining. This was advantageous in low-resource ASR applications, where only small amounts of speech data were available. Pretrained SSL models could be adapted with success at a low computational cost. However, due to the limited range of datasets SSL models were trained on, the fine-tuning results are not always consistent. Models often struggle to adapt to data not included or that is low-resource in pretraining \cite{FLEURS_XLSR_WHISPER}. As such, it is important that we understand which features in speech are learned during pretraining. We do this by first understanding the model itself.

In 2020, Baevski et al. \cite{wav2vec} published their work on the SSL ASR model, wav2vec 2.0. Wav2vec 2.0 builds on the transformer models outlined in Section~\ref{lr_subsec:transformer_in_speech} by using a CNN feature encoder and a transformer context network. Many previous ASR models used mel-spectrograms as an input to their first layers \cite{amodei2016deep, chorowski2015attention, hannun2014deep} The wav2vec 2.0 feature encoder takes a different approach by learning directly from the raw waveform. This allows the CNNs to learn latent speech features without being guided by the features already emphasised in a mel-spectrogram. The feature encoder consists of 7 1D CNN layers followed by batch normalisation \cite{pmlr-v37-ioffe15} and a GELU activation function \cite{hendrycks2016gaussian}.

The outputs of the feature encoder are then randomly masked with a similar approach to specaugment \cite{park2019specaugment} and then fed into the context network. The wav2vec 2.0 context network consists of 12 transformer blocks \cite{vaswani2017attention} for the "base" version and 24 for the "large"\cite{wav2vec}. Each block has a hidden layer size of 786 for base and 1024 for large. For positional encoding wav2vec 2.0 uses a single 1D convolutional layer \cite{lecuncnn1998} to encode a relative positional embedding rather than absolute positional embeddings \cite{vaswani2017attention}. The output of each block is then fed through the network and output at the final block as either a 786 or 1024 dimensional hidden state.

For self-supervised pretraining wav2vec 2.0 uses a contrastive loss function that compares the output of the context network with quantized speech representations. The quantizer first separates the latent features from the feature encoder before masking. The latent features are compared to codebooks of learned latent speech vectors, called codebook entries. A codebook entry with the highest similarity to the output of the feature encoder is selected from each codebook using the Gumbel softmax function \cite{jang2017categorical}. These selected entries are concatenated to form the contrastive vector. Several distractor vectors are also created, that do not relate to the input features. This concatenated vector and the distractors are then compared to the output of the context network. The network's ability to recognise the real latent features, is used to calculate the contrastive loss before backpropagation \cite{rumelhart1986learning}.\thoughts{Naomi Note: "Diagram on contrastive loss?". To understand what information wav2vec 2.0 learns, it is important to understand the loss function used in pretraining. Wav2vec 2.0 has two learning objectives in its loss function $\mathcal{L}$: contrastive loss $\mathcal{L}_m$ and diversity loss $\mathcal{L}_d$. 

\begin{equation}\label{lr_equ:wav2vec_loss}
    \mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_m + \alpha \mathcal{L}_d.
\end{equation}

Where $\alpha$ is a tuned hyperparameter used to weight diversity loss.

$\mathcal{L}_m$ is calculated as the minus log ratio between the output of the context network and the quantizer as seen in equation~\ref{lr_equ:contrastive_loss}. The numerator is the exponential, $\exp$, of the cosine similarity $sim$ between the output of the context network, $c_t$, at the masked time step $t$ and the true quantized speech representation $q_t$ at the same timestep. The denominator is the summation of the exponentials of the cosine similarity of $c_t$ and the set of quantized candidate speech representations $\tilde q_t$. The vector $\tilde q$ contains all distractor candidates as well as the true quantized representation, $q_t$. Here $\kappa$ is used as a temperature control that maximises or minimises the difference between the true target $c_t$ and quantized target $q_t$. Cosine similartity is calculated as: $sim(x,y)=\frac{x^Ty}{||x||||y||}$.  

\begin{equation}\label{lr_equ:contrastive_loss}
    \mathcal{L}_m = -\log\frac{\exp(sim(c_t,q_t)/\kappa)}{\sum_{\tilde q \in Q_t}\exp(sim(c_t,\tilde q_t)/\kappa)}
\end{equation}


$q_t$ is a vector of concatenated learned quantised latent speech representations extracted from the wav2vec 2.0 codebooks. Each codebook entry consists of a trainable layer, a fraction of the size of the output vector from the feature encoder. For each batch, the similarity between the output logits for each of $V$ codebook entries and the feature encoder output is calculated. The entries with the highest similarity from each of $G$ codebooks are selected and concatenated to create $q_t$. This creates a vector containing the most similar quantised speech latents to the input.

\begin{equation}\label{lr_equ:w2v2codebook}
    p_{g,v} = \frac{\exp(l_{g,v} + n_v)/\tau}{\sum_{u=1}^{V} \exp(l_{g,k} + n_k)/\tau}
\end{equation}

In equation \ref{lr_equ:w2v2codebook} we see the formula for selecting the highest similarity codebook entry $p_{g,v}$. The codebook has $V$ entries and $G$ total codebooks, $p_{g,v}$ is the score for the codeword at point $v$, codebook $g$. It is a ratio of the exponential $exp$ of the logits, with added noise $n_v$, at each entry over the sum of all of the $k$ logits and all noise, across the codebook. Both numerator and denominator are also divided by a temperature term $\tau$.  High values for $\tau$ encourage softer gradients and learning of broader features. While low values of tau increase the gradient to encourage specialisation for learned latent features

The added noise, $n_v$, uses a Gumbel distribution \cite{jang2017categorical}. It is calculated as $n=-log(-log(u))$ where $u$ are uniform Gumbel noise samples. Gumbel noise makes the logits function differentiable for backpropagation. It is stochastic noise added before the selection process. This increases entropy and helps prevent the model from converging towards a small set of codebook entries. The softmax function \cite{jang2017categorical} is applied across all $V$ entries to select the highest similarity for each codebook. 

The candidate representations, $\tilde q_t$, include the true latent speech $q_t$ and $K$ distractors, $Q_t$. The $K$ distractors are quantised speech latents that are not matched to the input through the Gumbel softmax function. Equation \ref{lr_equ:contrastive_loss} is comparing the $sim$ of $c_t$ and $q_t$ with the $sim$ of $c_t$ and $\tilde q_t$. This gives it a measure of how close the model output is to the real latent speech input.

\begin{equation}\label{lr_equ:diversity_loss}
    \mathcal{L}_d=\frac{1}{GV}\sum_{g=1}^G\sum_{v=1}^V\bar{p}_{g,v}log\bar{p}_{g,v}
\end{equation}

To further increase codeword entropy, the loss function in equation \ref{lr_equ:wav2vec_loss} includes a diversity loss term $\mathcal{L}_d$. This is the average softmax distribution across all codewords in a training batch. The average softmax distribution is first calculated as, $\bar{p}_{g,v}=\frac{1}{B}\sum_{b=1}^Bp_{g,v}^b$ where $B$ is the total size of the batch and $b$ is the current utterance. This distribution, $\bar{p}_{g,v}$, is then multiplied by $log\bar{p}_{g,v}$ to calculate the entropy for that batch. The mean average of all $V$ entry entropies across all $G$ codebooks is the diversity loss, $\mathcal{L}_d$. In the final loss calculation \ref{lr_equ:wav2vec_loss}, this term is then multiplied by the temperature control $\alpha$. As training goes on, the codewords will become more diverse and specialised, so $\alpha$ can be reduced.


\subsection{Fine-tuning Hyperparameters}
The contrastive SSL objective learns by creating latent speech representations at the context network output that have high similarity to the input. To recognise semantic labels, wav2vec 2.0 requires a second stage of training before it can perform ASR tasks, the fine-tuning stage. The SSL stage has already learned latent speech information, so smaller datasets and shorter training time can be used in fine-tuning.

To fine-tune wav2vec 2.0, a single randomly initialised classifier layer is added after the context network. This layer maps the output of the context network to characters extracted from the dataset labels. The model is then trained using CTC Loss \cite{graves2006connectionist} as outlined in Section \ref{lr_subsec:ctc_were}. For fine-tuning the large models, two learning rates are tested: 2e-5 and 3e-5. They then select the learning rate that produced the lowest WER for the results published. The base model is always fine-tuned with a learning rate of 1e-4. 

For all models, the optimizer is Adam with a tri-state scheduler. The learning rate is warmed up for the first 10\% of updates, held for 40\% and then has a linear decay for the remaining 50\% of updates. The batch size for fine-tuning base is 3.2m samples per GPU, training with 8 GPUs. The batch size for fine-tuning large is 1.28m samples per GPU, training with 24 GPUs. They freeze learnable parameters across the entire model, except for the classifier layer, for the first 10k updates. For the remaining updates, the classifier and context network are unfrozen and trained. The feature encoder is frozen throughout the entire fine-tuning stage. They also mask the latent output of the feature encoder \cite{park2019specaugment}. For several of their experiments, Baevski et al. \cite{wav2vec} use a Language Model (LM). They use either a 4-gram LM or a 20-block transformer LM trained on the LibriSpeech LM corpus.

\subsection{Finetuning Results}
To test the relation between pretraining and fine-tuning hours, Baevski et al. \cite{wav2vec} fine-tune several models with varying amounts of data. They pretrain 3 models, base with 960h of Librispeech data (base-LS960), large with LibriSpeech (large-LS960) and large with 60k of LibriVox data (large-LV60k). The LibriVox data is taken from the same clean read speech data as LibriSpeech and LibriLight \cite{librispeech, librilight}. They fine-tune each model to 10 minute, 1 hour and 10 hour and then the 100 hour clean subsets of LibriSpeech \cite{librispeech}. These pretrained and finetuned models were made open source and are available on Hugging Face\footnote{\hyperlink{https://huggingface.co/docs/transformers/model_doc/wav2vec2}{huggingface.co/docs/transformers/model\_doc/wav2vec2}}.

They find that when fine-tuning to only 10 minutes of LibriSpeech data, all of their models are competitive with SOTA results. Previous SOTA models used supervised training throughout and achieved WER in the 5-6\% range. These wav2vec 2.0 results are obtained with the transformer LM. Without the LM, the WER is much higher for all models, at over 40\% for base. However, when fine-tuning on the 100 hour subset, the models perform much better. Both large models substantially outperform SOTA results even without an LM. Then, base-LS960 produces comparable WER to SOTA without and outperforms SOTA with the transformer LM. They find that large-LV60k can produce SOTA results without an LM, with as little as 10 hours of data.

If 100 hours of speech data are available for an application, the base model might be a viable option. However, for low-resource applications at 10 hours or less, an LM would be necessary. The transformer LM is 24 blocks in size, the same size as the large model context network, and requires training. This suggests that the number of trainable parameters is directly related to the model's performance. They test this hypothesis by comparing the large and base models when both are pretrained on the same LibriSpeech 960-hour dataset. For all combinations of fine-tuning data and LM, large always results in a lower WER than base. 


\subsection{Phoneme Recognition}
Baevski et al. \cite{wav2vec} also test phoneme accuracy for wav2vec 2.0. They test a model pretrained on LibriSpeech and finetuned on 10 hours of LibriLight \cite{librilight} without a language model. This model produces a PER of 8.3\% on the TIMIT dataset \cite{timit}, a dataset with hand-annotated phoneme intervals. This is an improvement over previous PER benchmarks \cite{wav2vec}. This shows that wav2vec 2.0 has not just learned semantic information through CTC, but also has learned phonetic information.

\section{Multilingual Self-Supervised Learning}\label{lr_sec:multilingual_ssl}
In this section, we will explore multilingual pretraining for the wav2vec 2.0 \cite{wav2vec} family of models by discussing XLSR-53 \cite{XLSR-53} and XLS-R \cite{babu2021xlsr}. We first compare results on multiple languages between these models and the monolingual wav2vec 2.0. We then look at which aspects of training and architecture help these models in learning multiple languages. We assess how different model sizes affect learning and how dataset size relates to accuracy. We then discuss the trade-off between multilingual, mono-lingual and related-language pretraining.

\subsection{Cross-Lingual Representations}
In the same year, members of the same Facebook AI team that published wav2vec 2.0 \cite{wav2vec} published a multilingual SSL ASR model, XLSR-53 \cite{XLSR-53}. Conneau et al. \cite{XLSR-53} adapt the wav2vec 2.0 architecture by developing their own strategy for pretraining on multilingual speech data. XLSR-53 encourages utterances from low-resource languages in pretraining to be upsampled in each batch. 

\begin{equation}
    p_l=(\frac{n_l}{N})^\alpha
\end{equation}

Here, $n_l$ is the number of pretraining hours in a language $l$, $N$ is the total number of hours in pretraining and $\alpha$ is the upsampling factor. A high $\alpha$, a maximum of 1, does not upsample low-resource languages with batches reflecting the overall balance of languages in the total pretraining data. A low $\alpha$ value increases the chance of a batch including more utterances from a low-resource language. 

First, Conneau et al. \cite{XLSR-53} test base and large models on monolingual vs multilingual pretraining. They pretrain XLSR-53 on 1.3k hours of Common Voice (CV) \cite{ardila-etal-2020-common} English. They fine-tune this model to 10 other languages and compare the results to 10 monolingual models, pretrained and fine-tuned on each of their respective languages. They test the PER of each model on small phoneme annotated CV subsets. They find that the English model achieves a lower average PER than monolingual models. They next pretrain a model with 1.3k hours of multilingual data. The dataset contains 793 hours from 10 languages and 557 hours from English. For the base model, pretraining with an $\alpha$ of 0.5 on mutlilingual data produces lower PER than an $\alpha$ of 1. This shows that the upsampling strategy is effective. Both multilingual models also have lower PER than any of the monolingual models.

However, the PER differs greatly between languages and appears to be related to the number of hours there are for a language. French, for example, has 353 hours and Spanish has 168 hours. Both of these languages achieve a lower PER on their monolingual base model than the English model. Conneau et al. \cite{XLSR-53} acknowledge that there is a "trade-off" with multilingual pretraining. This trade-off decreases error rates for low-resource languages, but increases them for high-resource languages. 

They also note that increasing the number of parameters in a model can increase performance. Pretraining the large model, 24 transformer blocks, on the same multilingual data further decreases the average PER. This strategy decreases PER for all languages, high or low-resource. However, pretraining and fine-tuning the base model, 12 transformer blocks, on a single high-resource language still outperforms the large multilingual model. 

The highest performing model for most languages is XLSR-53 itself \cite{XLSR-53}. XLSR-53 follows the same 24 block architecture as wav2vec 2.0 large. It is trained on 56k hours of data containing 53 unique languages. The datasets included in pretraining are: CV \cite{ardila-etal-2020-common}, BABEL \cite{gales2014speech}, and Multilingual LibriSpeech (MLS) \cite{MLS}. The exact value of $\alpha$ is not specified. When testing on both CV and BABEL, this model achieves the lowest PER for most languages.

Conneau et al \cite{XLSR-53} note that both the size of the model and the quantity of pretraining data are important factors in achieving accurate multilingual models. However, they also compare XLSR-53 to a wav2vec 2.0 large-LV60k \cite{wav2vec, librilight} a model of the same size, pretrained on a similar amount of English monolingual data. XLSR-53 achieves lower WER on every language except English. 

XLSR-53 also fails to achieve lower WER when compared to monolingual supervised models trained on MLS for 4 of the 8 languages in the dataset. Notably, these four languages could be considered high-resource within MLS, having 900 or more hours of data. Dutch here is the exception, having lower WER on XLSR-53 while being high-resource in MLS. The other 3 languages that perform better on XLSR-53 than their monolingual counterparts all have less than 250 hours.

They then test multilingual related-language pretraining, by mixing 5 hours of Italian data with 50 hours of data from 6 different languages separately. They find that the model pretrained with 55 hours of Italian data has the lowest error. The next lowest error is achieved by mixing 5 hours of Italian with 50 hours of Spanish data. The highest error comes from mixed Italian and Chinese speech. This shows that monolingual pretraining is most effective but that related-language pretraining can also improve performance. 

Finally, Conneau et al. \cite{XLSR-53} measure how well XLSR-53 has learned cross-lingual speech representations by examining its codebooks after pretraining. They run k-means clustering on discrete latents obtained from the quantizers of 3 multilingual models. The clusters show that the model shares discrete tokens for certain groups of languages. Romance languages Italian, Basque, Catalan and Spanish form one clear cluster, showing similarity between these languages. This may explain the previous results where Italian performed well when mixed with Spanish data. The Germanic languages English and German are closely clustered alongside French. French is classically a Romance language, but do share many linguistic features with English and German.

Language clusters appear to conform to other historical language groupings, such as Arabic and Kabyle or Mandarin and Cantonese. This may suggest that the model learns cross-lingual information well between related languages. However, outliers such as Hong Kong Cantonese forming its own cluster may also suggest otherwise. Conneau et al. \cite{XLSR-53} do not explore this further, but suggest that encouraging shared tokens between similar languages may improve cross-lingual transfer.


\subsection{Cross-Lingual Representations at Scale}
The following year, in 2021, Babu et al. \cite{babu2021xlsr} expanded further on the ideas of multilingual SSL learning with their model XLS-R. XLS-R is another model in the wav2vec 2.0 family following the same quantizer and context network architecture as wav2vec 2.0 \cite{wav2vec}. It also keeps the same multilingual pretraining strategy as XLSR-53 \cite{XLSR-53}. XLS-R instead changes the scale of the approach by increasing both the size of the models and the number of hours in pretraining data. XLS-R comes in 3 sizes: 0.3B, 1B and 2B parameters. For the 0.3B model, the context network has 24 transformer blocks with a layer size of 1024. For the 1B and 2B models the context network has 48 blocks. The 1B parameter model has a layer size of 1024 and the 2B model is expanded to 1920.

For Librispeech results from \cite{babu2021xlsr, librispeech} large-LV60k has lower WER than XLS-R 0.3B or 2B. The large-LV60k LibriLight finetuning \cite{wav2vec}, results in a higher WER than XLS-R 2B at 6.3\%. However, with 1.7B fewer parameters, this is still a comparable outcome. This result is also lower than the XLSR-53 8.3\% on LibriSpeech. When using the 24 block transformer LM, wav2vec 2.0 base has a WER of 3.2\% on LibriSpeech, lower than XLS-R 2B. Even with the extra trainable parameters in the LM, the monolingual model here still has fewer parameters than XLS-R 2B.

In 2024, Pratap et al. \cite{pratap2024scaling} extended the XLS-R multilingual experiment further still. They created a large dataset containing a total of 55k hours of multilingual labelled speech data. The dataset would contain 1,036 different languages, vastly more than XLS-R's 128 \cite{babu2021xlsr}. They created this dataset by taking new testament bible recordings by Faith Comes by Healing\footnote{\hyperlink{https://www.faithcomesbyhearing.com/}{faithcomesbyhearing.com}}.

They pretrain the 0.3B and 1B parameter versions of XLS-R by combining the same datasets previously used with XLS-R and the MMS-Lab-U unlabelled 55k-hour dataset (XLS-R-MMS). They find that when fine-tuned to the FLEURs \cite{FLEURS} dataset, XLS-R-MMS produced lower average CER than the 0.3B and 1B XLS-R models \cite{pratap2024scaling}. They also find that XLS-R-MMS had lower CER than XLS-R for languages that were low-resource in the XLS-R-MMS pretraining. XLS-R-MMS also had higher CER than XLS-R on languages that were high-resource in pretraining. 

They then pretrain multiple models with different numbers of languages from MMS: 61, 128, 256, 512 and 1107 languages. They then fine-tune each model to the entire FLEURs dataset and measure the average CER. They find that with each increase in the number of languages in pretraining, the average CER increases. They also find that the CER does not increase linearly. The increase in CER between 512 languages and 1107 languages is smaller than the increase between 61 and 128 languages. However, neither the specific languages nor the number of hours added with each step is reported, so the effect of these factors is unclear.

\subsection{Neural Scaling}
The results across these multilingual experiments show a consistent trade-off. That increasing the number of languages included in pretraining results in increased error for individual languages. High-resource languages especially benefit from monolingual pretraining and lose accuracy in multilingual settings. However, for languages that do not have enough data to effectively pretrain a monolingual model, this is not the case. For low-resource languages, multilingual pretraining can improve the downstream WER. 


This trade-off for high-resource languages can be offset by increasing the model parameter count. At 2B parameters, XLS-R can be competitive with monolingual models for high-resource languages, while still benefiting low-resource languages. However, the number of parameters required to achieve a similar low WER is much less in monolingual settings. Monolingual models with 20 times fewer parameters can achieve the same WER as XLS-R when finetuned to matched-language data. Here matched-language refers to when pretraining and fine-tuning languages match.

%\newpage
In 2017, Hestness et al. \cite{hestness2017deep} explored the relationship between model size, training data size and validation loss. They show for Deep Speech 2 that for the model with the largest parameter count, 87M, validation loss consistently fell as the number of hours in the training data increased. The relationship was linear until only the largest datasets, where the benefit of increasing the dataset size was reduced. For smaller models, the loss decrease plateaued at much smaller dataset sizes, showing that low-parameter count models did not benefit as much from very large datasets.

Figure \ref{lr_fig:scaling_laws} shows the relationship between training dataset size and model size. Here, small datasets struggle to reduce loss in the small-data region. The power-scaling region is where large models linearly benefit from increasing training data. The irreducible error region is where the benefits of increased training data reduce. Hestness et al. \cite{hestness2017deep} note that they did not find a threshold for data size that did not decrease loss compared to smaller sizes, just that the benefits were more when parameter count was high.

In 2020 Kaplan et al. \cite{kaplan2020scaling} further investigate scaling laws by attempting to find an optimal point between dataset size, model size and available compute size. They first train multiple models with different parameter counts on a language modelling task. Overall, the largest models at 708M parameters produced the lowest loss when trained on the largest dataset, of approximately 22B text tokens. However, when the smallest model was trained on the 22B token dataset, it outperformed the same small model trained on smaller datasets. Similarly, when a large model was trained on a small dataset, it outperformed small models trained on small datasets. 

The benefits of increasing model size seem to reduce as the dataset size is reduced. They plot the best loss for each dataset by the parameter count for each model. For the 22B token dataset loss kept reducing linearly without plateauing as the parameter count was increased. However, for the small 21M token dataset, loss plateaued after 85M parameters and even increased at 708M parameters. This shows that for small datasets, large model sizes can unecessary or even be detrimental. Whereas for large datasets, large model sizes are necessary.

Scaling research seems to support the findings by Babu et al. \cite{babu2021xlsr} that training on very large multilingual data requires a very large model size to effectively learn from. Radford et al. \cite{whisper} study the effects of parameter count and multilingual accuracy in depth for Whisper. They show that when Whisper performed zero-shot ASR, WER reduction scaled to model size. The lowest multilingual WER was produced with the 1.5B parameter model, the largest version of Whisper. Interestingly, for English, the largest data language in the training data, WER plateaus at 718M parameters. This appears to show that 1.5B is not necessary for this language. They also show that the largest multilingual Whisper models outperform smaller models trained only on English. However, smaller multilingual models produce significantly higher English WER than the same size monolingual model. 

For other languages in the Whisper training data \cite{whisper}, they plot zero-shot WER for FLEURs \cite{FLEURS} languages vs the number of hours in the training data. They find that the number of hours for a language that Whisper saw in training is directly correlated to WER. High-resource languages in training produced low WER, and low-resource languages produced high WER. The WER for each language scales with the number of hours in pretraining. Given these findings, we can see that multilingual pretraining for ASR has multiple dimensions that relate to good performance. The total amount of speech data in training, the parameter count and the balance of data between languages, all affect the final WER. 



\subsection{Monolingual or Multilingual}
We have shown so far that pretraining SSL models for multilingual ASR can benefit low-resource languages. We have seen the relationship between parameter counts and the total number of hours and languages in pretraining. So next, we must explore how the relationships between the pretraining languages and the fine-tuning languages affect performance.

In 2021, Poncelet et al. \cite{poncelet2021comparison} fine-tuned wav2vec 2.0 large-LV60k, to Flemish Dutch data. They found that it had a lower WER than the same model when pretrained on the Netherlands Dutch from the VoxPopuli \cite{wang-etal-2021-voxpopuli} dataset. The base-LS960 also achieved a lower WER than the Netherlands Dutch model and XLSR-53 had the lowest WER overall. These results seem to confirm that multilingual SSL ASR learned enough cross-lingual information to adapt to a new low-resource language. Here, the related language, Netherlands Dutch, was less beneficial to downstream WER than multilingual pretraining. However, Netherlands and Flemish Dutch are distinct dialects. They each have many different pronunciations of common words \cite{ipahandbook_1999}, which could have contributed to the Flemish results.

\section{Model Compression}\label{lr_sec:model_compression}
So far in this literature review, we have explored the benefits of pretraining SSL models. Knowledge learned in pretraining can benefit low-resource languages when fine-tuning. Including data from a matched or related language in pretraining can improve performance further. However, as pretraining data size increases, the size of the model must increase to learn it effectively. Fine-tuning large models, even on small datasets, can still be computationally costly. In this section, we explore the concept of model compression, focusing on The Lottery Ticket Hypothesis (LTH) and model pruning. Model compression techniques aim to reduce the size of large models while retaining high performance. We will explore how some of these techniques have been used in deep learning and how they have been applied to speech.  

\subsection{Model Compression}
Model compression is an area of study that aims to reduce the size of large pretrained models while preserving their accuracy. There are multiple active areas of research in model compression, such as low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) \cite{hu2021lora}. LoRA \cite{hu2021lora} is a technique that inserts trainable layers between the layers or blocks of a pretrained model. The pretrained weights are frozen and only the new layers are trained. This allows the model to benefit from pretrained weights while training the fewest possible parameters.

Model distillation \cite{distillation} is another common compression technique. Distillation uses a large pretrained model to teach a smaller model from scratch. Hidden state activations from one or multiple layers of the large model are used to create a loss value. The small model is trained to emulate these weight activations for a given dataset. However, while the larger model is frozen during training, this technique can still be computationally expensive. 

In this section, we will focus on The Lottery Ticket Hypothesis (LTH) and model pruning \cite{han2015learning, frankle2018LTH}. This approach to compression aims to find the highest-value weights in a large model. They then discard as many low-value weights as possible, while retaining relative performance. High-value weights are learned parameters that contribute the most to downstream training. For our purposes, isolating the weights that contribute to different speech tasks is valuable when studying SSL ASR. 

\subsection{The Lottery Ticket Hypothesis}

In 2019, Frankle et al. \cite{frankle2018LTH} published their work on what they called The Lottery Ticket Hypothesis (LTH). LTH states that: "A randomly-initialized, dense neural network contains a subnetwork that is initialized such that, when trained in isolation, it can match the test accuracy of the original network after training for at most the same number of iterations" \cite{frankle2018LTH}. They refer to these subnetworks as ``winning tickets''. They hypothesise that these tickets, if found, are much more efficient for training to high accuracy than training the entire model. These winning tickets are not specific weights that can be found before a network is trained. When a model is randomly initialised, subnetworks do not carry over, they must be found again after a DNN has been trained. 

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth,trim={0 130 0 0},clip]{2_chapter2/images/model_compression/structured_unstructured_pruning.pdf}
    \caption{An illustration of structured and unstructured pruning. Left depicts a fully connected DNN before pruning. Center depicts a DNN with unstructured pruning, here specific weight are removed surign pruning. Right depicts structured pruning where nodes and all the connected weights are removed during pruning.}
    \label{lr_fig:unstructured_structured_pruning}
\end{figure}

The neural scaling principle tells us that increasing the number of parameters in a DNN can lead to higher accuracy. It also leads to more retention of information learned from large training datasets \cite{kaplan2020scaling}. Having more trainable parameters in a network allows more weights to become specialised to a certain task. However, specialised weights can be redundant when fine-tuning to a specific domain that does not require them. LTH capitalises on this by pruning a large network of its lowest value individual weights. By reducing a network to only its high value weights, only subnetworks containing very generalisable weights are left. Fine-tuning these subnetworks alone is shown to have comparable accuracy to fine-tuning the entire model.

There are multiple approaches to finding winning tickets in DNNs, such as unstructured pruning \cite{frankle2018LTH} and structured pruning \cite{li2017pruning, han2015learning}. Unstructured pruning selects the highest value weights across a selected region. This is most commonly applied layer-wise or globally across the whole model. A target sparsity is set and that percentage of the lowest value weights will be removed from either a layer or the whole model. Sparsity is the target percentage of weights to prune. If the sparsity is set to 60\%, then only 40\% of weights will remain after pruning. When applying sparsity layer-wise 60\% unstructured sparsity would prune 60\% of the weights in every layer of a model. If sparsity were applied globally, then 60\% of the weights across the model would be removed.

However, removing individual weights with unstructured pruning requires dedicated hardware or software libraries to achieve \cite{cheng2024survey}. GPUs are not optimised to compute the irregular matrix shapes that unstructured pruning creates for training and inference. Structured pruning solves this problem by pruning whole neurons within the model, such as in \cite{han2015learning}. Removing entire neurons and their connected weights is an easier task to implement in hardware. Pruning a neuron removes an entire row in each of the matrix calculations in the forward and backwards passes. Structured pruning, therefore, requires no extra optimisation for GPU training or inference. The trade-off here, however, is that structured pruning achieves lower final accuracies when compared to unstructured pruning \cite{mao2017exploring, yvinec2021red}. Due to its broader approach to finding winning tickets, individual high-value weights are more likely to be discarded. 

Structured pruning can also be abstracted further by pruning whole layers in a model. Studies have shown that certain layers across a DNN can contribute more than others to the final accuracy of a model \cite{gromov2024unreasonable}. Layer pruning is even more computationally efficient and simpler to implement than unstructured weight pruning. However, layer pruning abstracts the pruning even further from the individual high-value weights. So, accuracy is most negatively impacted by this technique when compared to weight or neuron pruning.

The most common approach to finding winning tickets is a task-aware approach by pruning after fine-tuning a pretrained model. This is referred to as One-Shot Magnitude Pruning (OMP). Pruning a pretrained model before finetuning can reduce computational costs. However, this subnetwork will be task-agnostic and may not generalise well to your downstream task. By fine-tuning the pretrained model before pruning, the fine-tuned weights become task-specific, which have been shown to produce more accurate results \cite{frankle2018LTH, PARP}.

Iterative Magnitude Pruning (IMP) says that the most efficient subnetwork may form over multiple iterations of pruning. IMP prunes a percentage of the target sparsity between each stage of fine-tuning \cite{frankle2018LTH}. For example, if the target sparsity was 50\% and the model was fine-tuned for 5 epochs 10\% of the model's total weights could be pruned at the end of every epoch. This approach would reach the target sparsity of 50\% after epoch 5 had completed. Pruning with this approach allows the remaining network to readjust its weights before more connections are removed. IMP aims to uncover weights or neurons that might otherwise be removed with blunter approaches to pruning. OMP and IMP are, so far, the most effective approaches tested for finding winning tickets in a DNN \cite{frankle2018LTH, liu2018rethinking, You2020Drawing, PARP}.

\subsection{Language-Specific Subnetworks}
Several studies have shown that task-aware subnetworks obtained using LTH on large multilingual ASR models contain weights learned for a specific language. These language-specific subnetworks can be found by fine-tuning a multilingual model to a single language before pruning. The resulting network will contain a proportion of weights that do not overlap with other language-specific subnetworks. 

In 2021, Lai et al. \cite{PARP} studied language-specific networks in wav2vec 2.0 \cite{wav2vec} and XLSR-53 \cite{XLSR-53}. They finetune wav2v2ec 2.0 and XLSR-53 to multiple monolingual sets from 10 languages and apply OMP to extract the subnetworks. To understand how these language-specific subnetworks interact, they calculate the Intersection Over Union (IOU) as seen in Equation~\ref{lr_equ:IOU}. 

\begin{equation}\label{lr_equ:IOU}
    IOU(m^a, M^b)\hat = \frac{|(m^a=1)\cap(m^b=1)|}{|(m^a=1)\cup(m^b=1)}
\end{equation}

The IOU calculates the number of weights that overlap between two LTH subnetworks in a model. They find that the IOU between language-specific subnetworks in both models and a randomly selected pruning strategy is consistently low at 33.33\%. For LTH subnetworks from different languages in wav2vec 2.0, the IOU was always greater than 96\%. For subnetworks in XLSR-53 IOU was always greater than or equal to 93\%. They also compare the IOUs for language-specific subnetworks and the task-agnostic Magnitude Pruning at Pre-trained Initialisations (MPI). For both models, all languages had similar, >90\%, IOUs with the MPI subnetwork, although they don't report the exact results. 

They claim that these results show that MPI networks are more efficient for pruning. They designed a pruning technique that they named Prune Adjust and Re-Prune (PARP). PARP is based on iterative pruning starting from the MPI and using as few iterations as possible. PARP is able to produce good results, when compared to language-specific subnetworks, with only 2 epochs of training.

Lai et al. \cite{PARP} compare the IOUs generated between the MPI subnetworks and IMP and OMP subnetworks. They find that at 50\% sparsity, IMP and OMP subnetworks have over 90\% IOU with each MPI subnetwork. They also find that IMP and OMP IOUs with MPI increase as sparsity increases. They then fine-tune a wav2vec 2.0 model for phoneme recognition to test subnetwork overlap. They measure the relative change between PER when different language-specific subnetworks are used to prune the model. They find that the relative PER change is high among language-specific OMP subnetworks that do not match. For example, when wav2vec 2.0 is recognising Dutch phonemes and pruned with the Russian OMP subnetwork, PER has a 7.78\% relative increase. 

It is notable that when pruning a wav2vec 2.0 with OMP there are, in some cases, stark increases in the relative PER. This suggests that the small number of weights that do not overlap between language-specific subnetworks can have a substantial impact on downstream performance. The appendix \cite{PARP} also shows that the IOU is lower for XLSR-53 language-specific subnetworks, when compared to wav2vec 2.0 subnetworks. This may suggest that multilingual models learn to specialise more of their weights to language-specific tasks. It is also notable that they do not produce or assess an English language-specific subnetwork. English is the most high-resource language in both wav2vec 2.0 and XLSR-53. So, it may be beneficial to look at the IOUs between an English subnetwork and the MPI or other subnetworks.

In 2022, Lu et al. \cite{Lu_Language_adap_xlsr} tested joint training with multiple language-specific subnetworks. They pretrain their own XLSR model using the base and large models from \cite{XLSR-53}. They pretrain both base and large XLSR models on multilingual CV data \cite{ardila-etal-2020-common} for 250k updates for base and 400k for large, XLSR-10. They then continue pretraining for 50k updates on a single language subset of CV. Finally, they perform unstructured pruning on the resulting model in order to extract the language-specific subnetworks. They also extract a task-agnostic, multilingual subnetwork by pruning the pretrained multilingual model. They then continue pretraining on the multilingual data for 50k steps. All subnetworks are extracted at 40\% sparsity. After testing multiple sparsities, 40\% is found to be the optimal sparsity to produce the lowest PER.

They fine-tune each pruned model to 1 hour of CV data and then measure each subnetwork for PER. They find that language-specific subnetworks produce lower PER than the multilingual subnetwork and XLSR-10 without pruning. Language-specific subnetworks also produce lower PER than all but three of the monolingual models trained in \cite{XLSR-53}. Spanish, French and Italian monolingual base models all produce lower PER than their language-specific subnetworks extracted from XLSR-10. As seen previously \cite{XLSR-53}, these high-resource languages in CV produce monolingual models that perform well for phoneme recognition. 

Here, it appears that language-specific subnetworks are particularly beneficial for low-resource languages. These results contradict the findings of Lai et al. \cite{PARP}, that language-agnostic pruning is the best approach for downstream error. Although this technique is more computationally intensive than PARP \cite{PARP, Lu_Language_adap_xlsr}.

Finally, in 2023, Yang et al. \cite{yang2023learning} show that language-specific subnetworks produced from structured pruning have better performance than language-agnostic approaches. They first pretrain multiple monolingual RNN-Tranducer (RNN-T) \cite{graves2012rnnt} models on 4 of the languages from MLS \cite{MLS}. They prune these models using structured pruning with either IMP or OMP. They prune to 70\% sparsity and identify the monolingual winning tickets for each language. They then use the subnetworks found on each monolingual model for all of their experiments. They also pretrain a multilingual RNN-T model using data from all 4 languages. They train on monolingual batches and only update weights in the subnetwork related to the language in the current batch.

They first compare a monolingual model with 30M parameters to a monolingual model with 100M parameters, but pruned to 70\% sparsity. They find that fine-tuning on a language-specific subnetwork results in lower WER than fine-tuning the entire 30M parameter model. It also has comparable or lower WER to a multilingual 100M parameter model without any pruning. Their multilingual model, trained with language-specific subnetworks, also achieves comparable WER to the monolingual models. 

They show through IOU that their monolingual subnetworks have low overlap with an approximate range of 30\% to 50\%. This is in contrast to previous IOU measurements \cite{PARP}. This effect is likely due to the language-specific subnetworks being drawn from separately pretrained models. Using a subnetwork from a separate monolingual model does not guarantee that the winning ticket will transfer across, due to the random initialisation. Their findings support this by showing that randomly selected subnetworks produce only marginally higher WER.

This research shows that LTH can reveal language-specific subnetworks within the weights of a large pretrained multilingual model. Fine-tuning or continued pretraining on the subnetworks can increase monolingual performance and reduce computational costs. However, little work has been done to analyse the information contained in these subnetworks. It has been shown that subnetworks obtained from the same model have high overlap. Some subnetworks also have different performance when fine-tuning to matched or mismatched languages. However, linguistic relationships between language-specific subnetworks have not, so far, been studied. Neither has the relationship between language-specific subnetworks and the balance of languages in pretraining data. 

In Chapter~\ref{chapter4} of this thesis, we address these gaps in knowledge by studying the content of language-specific subnetworks in XLS-R \cite{babu2021xlsr}. We use language-specific subnetworks to understand how the balance of languages in the pretraining data affects its fine-tuned performance. We also examine how these subnetworks relate to one another through IOU. We study whether linguistic relationships between languages relate to language-specific subnetworks. With these contributions, we identify the most efficient pruning strategy for XLS-R.


\section{Model Analysis}\label{lr_sec:model_analysis}
Model compression techniques are useful for understanding weight distributions in large ASR models. However, language-specific subnetworks still leave many questions about multilingual SSL unanswered. In order to better take advantage of SSL pretraining, we need to look further into how the models learn. If we can understand how SSL speech models learn language, we may be able to design better approaches to pretraining. 

In this section, we cover prominent papers that have studied the information contained within pretrained SSL ASR models. These studies relate learned weights to linguistic research on speech perception. They study how each layer, or each learned weight, in a model learns different elements of speech perception and transcription. By understanding how SSL ASR models perceive speech, we can improve strategies for pretraining SSL models.

\subsection{Layer-wise Analysis of wav2vec 2.0}
In 2021, Pasad et al. \cite{pasad2021layer} analysed the inner workings of wav2vec 2.0. They assess the performance of each layer of wav2vec 2.0 in multiple domains: local acoustic features, phone identity, word identity and word meaning. They use Canonical Correlation Analysis (CCA) as a statistical measure of the relationship between layer embeddings for a window of speech data and another vector. When CCA is high, there is a high correlation between the layer embedding vector and the test domain vector. These vectors contain linear projections of each of the test domains.

Pasad et al. \cite{pasad2021layer} test two pretrained wav2vec 2.0 models\footnote{\hyperlink{https://github.com/facebookresearch/fairseq/tree/main/examples/wav2vec}{github.com/facebookresearch/fairseq/tree/main/examples/wav2vec}\label{lr_la_fn:w2v2}}: base-LS960 and large-LV60k. They first compare model layers when wav2vec 2.0 is fine-tuned to different amounts of LibriSpeech data \cite{librispeech}: pretrained, 10m, 100h and 960h. They compare the output of the feature encoder to the outputs of each block in the context network. For the pretrained model, both base and large models have high CCA with the feature encoder in shallow layers. CCA then lowers during the middle layers for both models. CCA then rises in deep layers for the pretrained model, but not the finetuned models. 

They label the pretrained CCA pattern as "auto-encoder style behaviour" \cite{pasad2021layer, hinton2006reducing}, due to the rise in CCA in the deep layers. This behaviour may be an artefact of the contrastive loss function. It teaches the context network to learn the codebook entries that have the highest similarity to the feature encoder output. Fine-tuned models exhibit less of the autoencoder behaviour. The more data that is included in fine-tuning, the lower the CCA to the feature encoder is in deep blocks, blocks 9-12 in base and 18-24 in large. The fine-tuning loss function is CTC Loss, so the learning objective is character labels. The fine-tuned deep layers reflect this change in learning objective.

This is further confirmed when comparing hidden states from the feature encoder and the context network to mel-spectrograms. They show that the final layer of the feature encoder shows the highest similarity to mel-spectrograms of the same frame. CCA is highest in the shallow layers of the context network and decreases for the middle and deep layers. Latent speech representations are therefore shown to resemble the linguistic features highlighted in mel-spectrograms. This also seems to show that the model is learning broad linguistic information at shallow layers. While deep layers learn more task-specific information.

    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth,trim={0 40 0 60},clip]{2_chapter2/images/model_analysis/Layer-Wise_Analysis_cropped_graph.pdf}
    \caption{An approximation of the similarity for each test domain at each layer of wav2vec 2.o. Test domains include: local acoustic features, phone identity, word identity and word meaning. Similarity scores are normalised for visualisation purposes. Taken from (Pasad et al. 2021).}
    \label{lr_fig:language_specific_subnetworks}
\end{figure}

The context network is not only learning mel-spectrum information, but appears to learn phonetic and acoustic features. They use the Montreal Forced Aligner (MFA) \cite{mcauliffe2017montreal} to identify phoneme time intervals within each utterance. They collect frames within the phoneme interval and take the average of the representations across all frames. They exclude the first and final third of frames within the interval to avoid coarticulation. They also test Acoustically Grounded Word Embeddings (AGWE).  Word intervals are also found with MFA, by collecting and averaging all of the frames within this word interval, they form the AGWE. This gives an acoustic representation of each word. They then test the CCA at each layer output of both the pretrained and fine-tuned models.

They find that CCA between hidden states and phonemes is low in shallow layers, highest in middle layers and then reduces again at deep layers. This pattern is true for both pretrained and fine-tuned models. For the shallow and middle layers, the CCA score is very close between pretrained and finetuned base models. For deep layers, the fine-tuned base model has a higher CCA. This is true for large-LV60k as well. However, it is only the final block that shows a significant difference in CCA between loss objectives. These results suggest the model does not forget the phonetic information learned in pretraining. Therefore, phonetic information learned in pretraining is transferable to the fine-tuning task.

A similar pattern can be seen for AGWEs when analysing the pretrained model. However, for the fine-tuned model, CCA continues to rise in the deepest layers. This may explain the low AGWE CCA in the deep layers before fine-tuning. We know that wav2vec 2.0 codebook entries correlate with specific phonemes found in the pretraining data \cite{wav2vec}. So, contrastive loss is encouraging the model to learn phoneme representations. This may be why fine-tuning does not diverge much from already learned phonetic information. Word embeddings, however, improve after fine-tuning, because the SSL objective does not include fully realised words, but CTC does.

Finally, they assess the similarity layer outputs from the same models before and after fine-tuning. They show that for shallow and middle layers, there is high CCA, showing little change in the weight distribution. However, the deepest layers, 10-12 for base and 20-24 for large, show reduced CCA. This suggests that shallow and middle layers are learning linguistic information that is transferable between auto-encoder and ASR tasks. The deepest layers learn task-specific information in both pretraining and fine-tuning. They therefore change the most between training objectives.


In 2023, Pasad et al. \cite{pasad2023comparative} published a follow-up study, this time comparing CCA among multiple SSL speech models. They again test CCA similarity for local, mel-spectrogram, phone (CCA-phone) and word features (CCA-word). They assess CCA on 4 models trained on contrastive loss: wav2vec 2.0 \cite{wav2vec}, XLSR-53 \cite{XLSR-53}, FaST-VGS5 \cite{fastvgspeng2022}, FaST-VGS+ \cite{peng2022self}. They also assess CCA for 3 models trained with comparative loss: HuBERT \cite{hsu2021hubert}, WavLM \cite{chen2022wavlm}, AV-HuBERT\cite{shi2022learning}. They find that all of the contrastive loss models show the same auto-encoder style behaviour as seen previously in wav2vec 2.0 \cite{pasad2021layer}. The CNN feature encoders also show high similarity to mel-spectrogram features in contrastive models. However, the comparative loss does not show these same behaviours. Instead, CCA-phone and CCA-word both keep increasing towards the deepest layers.

They also test how CCA scores relate to performance in downstream tasks. They test whether CCA-phone or CCA-word scores relate to Phoneme Recognition (PR) and ASR tasks. They extract features from each layer of the context networks and train SuPERB models \cite{yang2021superb} for PR and ASR tasks. They find that for contrastive loss SSL models, CCA-phone correlates to both high PER and WER for the ASR task. CCA-word also correlates, but to a lesser extent. This is likely due to contrastive loss not containing a semantic objective. The layers with the highest CCA-phone are the middle transformer blocks in the context network. These experiments show that phonetic information learned in pretraining is directly relevant to the finetuning task. It is therefore important to understand how SSL models encode the phonetic features learned in pretraining.

    \caption{Left: average phoneme accuracy for each layer of wav2vec 2.0 pretrained on LibriSpeech. Middle: average accuracy for each manner of articulation class. Right: average accuracy for each place of articulation class. Adapted from: (English et al. 2022).}

With the findings of Pasad et al. \cite{pasad2021layer}, we see that pretraining effectively encodes phoneme information. The next step, therefore, is to understand if SSL ASR learns further abstracted speech units, such as articulations.

In 2022, English et al. \cite{english2022domain} collected phoneme accuracy data by training Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) probes for phoneme recognition. Each MLP consists of a single linear layer of input size 768, an output of 200, a ReLU activation function and a final layer with an output dimension of 1. They use the TIMIT dataset, which has time aligned, hand annotated phoneme intervals, to train the probes. Latent phoneme representations are collected from each layer of the wav2vec 2.0 encoder for each window of audio in the TIMIT utterances. For each annotated phoneme, the first and final third of the representations are discarded in order to control for coarticulation. Coarticulation is the overlap in articulations between two neighbouring phonemes in a word. A dataset of each of these phoneme realisations is collected and each probe is then trained for a single task.


First, they recreate the "auto-encoder style" behaviour when measuring the average accuracy across all phonemes at all layers. For the manner and place of articulation, they find that layer 7, the 7th transformer block of the context network, has robust results. Both of these results line up with the findings of Pasad et al. \cite{pasad2021layer}. When measuring Manner of Articulation (MoA) they find that accuracy varies across MoA classes. MoA classes group phonemes by the manner in which the tongue, lips, and vocal tract modify airflow to produce them. Vowels and fricatives have the highest average for MoA classes across all layers. Alveolar then has the highest average accuracy for Place of Articulation (PoA) classes. PoA classes group phonemes by the location in the vocal tract where airflow is obstructed or modified to produce them. All classes are found to have the highest accuracy at layer 7. They then generate confusion matrices and plot both heatmaps and dendrograms to measure phoneme similarity. They find that the phoneme representations at layers 0, 7 and 12 show most similarity to similar phonemes, such as those that share the same MoA class. This similarity is most clear at layer 7.

English et al. \cite{english2023discovering} later published follow-up studies that studied how phonetic features (such as voicing, frication and nasality \cite{ipahandbook_1999}) are encoded in wav2vec 2.0 \cite{wav2vec}. They again probe the model and measure TIMIT phoneme accuracies. They show that frication is well encoded across shallow and middle layers, dropping in accuracy in deep layers. Voicing is best encoded in the middle layers, with the highest accuracy at layer 8. Finally, Nasals have consistently high accuracy across all layers.

Separately from this work, Bosch et al. \cite{ten2023phonemic} also probed wav2vec 2.0 for phonetic features and feature relationships. They found that, generally, probes were an effective approach to analysing SSL models, achieving high accuracy across layers. When analysing phonetic features and phoneme labels, they found that the middle layers identified individual phonemes clearly. Shallow layers were less able to distinguish between individual phonemes, but could recognise Broad Phonetic Classes (BPCs). BPCs: plosives, nasals, approximants, fricatives, high vowels, mid vowels, and low vowels. Shallow layers often mislabelled individual phonemes, but were able to distinguish well between BPCs.


\subsection{Phonetic Features in SSL}
In 2022, Wells et al. \cite{wells2022phonetic} analysed HuBERT transformer blocks and how they related to MoA classes. They create k-means clusters derived from the outputs of layer 6 of the transformer encoder. The outputs are extracted from the LibriSpeech 100 hour clean subset \cite{librispeech}. They test 4 k-means models with 50, 100, 250 and 500 units. They test phones for unit purity similar to the experiments in \cite{hsu2021hubert} and extrapolate this to MoA classes. The test phone purity for units with the multi-accented English first language, L1, speech from the VCTK corpus of English speech \cite{yamagishi2019cstr}.

%Wells et al. \cite{wells2022phonetic} find that phone purity is low at a model size of 50. Purity increases as the number of units increases, with the 500 units model having the highest purity. However, unit purity decreases with an increase in model size. These results suggest that as model size increases more units cluster to single phonemes. The number of differently realised phonemes aligned to a cluster, but the units are more likely to represent parts of different phonemes. This aligns with the claims in \cite{hsu2021hubert} that units represent articulations rather than phonemes directly. 

They find that k-means units align to aspiration, closure and release bursts, among other articulations. Monophthong vowels, vowels consisting of a single phoneme, are relatively low in purity, meaning each phoneme often shares many units. However, the phonemes that share these units are closely related, either similar vowels or voiced consonants with the same articulations. Both the manner and place of articulation across vowels are generally well represented by the clusters. Diphthongs are a single vowel sound created by gliding between the articulations of two separate vowels \cite{ipahandbook_1999}. They find that units can be assigned to specific diphthongs and even represent the gliding coarticulation. These findings show that contrastive SSL loss can also embed clear phonetic features. In 2023, Sicherman et al. \cite{sicherman2023analysingdiscrete} confirm many of the findings from Wells et al. \cite{wells2022phonetic} with their visualisation of k-means units. They collect representations from HuBERT \cite{hsu2021hubert} and train k-means on the LibriSpeech test100-clean subset \cite{librispeech}. When visualising unit clusters, they find that units associated with the same articulations cluster together. 

English et al. \cite{english2023discovering} also study the co-occurrence of phonetic features. Exclusive features that can not co-occur, such as articulations, are often considered mutually exclusive by wav2vec 2.0. However, features such as frication and voicing often co-occur. Voicing is not mutually exclusive with any articulation, and wav2vec 2.0 seems to have learned this. Feature co-occurrences are shown in their later paper \cite{english2024searching} to follow similar patterns to phonemeic associations found in linguistics studies \cite{sagey1986representation}. These associations were found in wav2vec 2.0 by probing layer 9 of the context network. In their final paper on probing wav2vec 2.0 \cite{wav2vec}, English et al. \cite{english2024following} also show that wav2vec 2.0 can accurately detect coarticulation. 

\subsection{Phonetic Features in the Quantizer}\label{lr_subsec:analysis_quantizer}
From the wav2vec 2.0 appendix \cite{wav2vec}, we know that many of the codebook entries in the quantizer correlated to specific phonemes. This suggests that the wav2vec 2.0 quantizer also converges towards specific phonetic features. In 2023, Abdullah et al. \cite{abdullah2023information} analysed wav2vec 2.0 and XLSR-53 codebook entries for phoneme entropy. They ran the quantizers for each model on TIMIT data \cite{timit} to assess how many entries were activated across every phoneme in the dataset. If a phoneme activated a large number of entries across multiple realisations, it could be considered to have high entropy. They find that phonemes are more entropic in wav2vec 2.0 than XLSR-53. For wav2vec 2.0, 56.6\% of the entries had a non-zero count for phoneme activations, compared to just 24.2\% of XLSR-53 entries. 

They also find that no phoneme is minimally entropic, none activate on a single entry. They then find that vowels are more entropic than consonants. Nasals, approximants and fricatives are the most entropic consonants. Within this finding, voiced fricatives are more entropic than voiceless fricatives. Affricates are found to be the least entropic category. Finally, they perform hierarchical clustering on quantized representations for each phoneme. They find for both models, most phoneme categories cluster towards other phonemes in their MoA class. Approximant and nasals form the closest clusters to vowels. This suggests that vowels share much of their latent space both within the class and between other, similar, MoA classes.

These results are revealing when assessing the information the wav2vec 2.0 framework learns during pretraining. However, some information is still missing. It is notable that XLSR-53 has lower overall entropy than wav2vec 2.0, and that the TIMIT dataset only contains English realisations of each phoneme. In the original XLSR-53 paper \cite{XLSR-53}, it was found that the frequency distributions for selected entries could be language-specific, as seen in Figure~\ref{lr_fig:xlsr_53_multilingual_quantizer}. These distributions correlated with language families, with little overlap between unrelated languages. This could explain the low entropy for XLSR-53 on TIMIT phonemes. It is possible that multiple entries are being used for the same phonetic feature realised in different languages. In this case, testing with monolingual data might produce a low entropy score. If true, low cross-lingual entropy might contribute to the increase in downstream error shown on individual languages for multilingual models. 

However, so far, no study has provided definitive evidence for this hypothesis. There has been no study on a multilingual quantizer that proves that entries are not shared. Neither has there been a definitive study on how well multilingual SSL ASR models learn multilingual phonemes or phonetic features. There has also been little work on quantifying how language balance in multilingual pretraining impacts overall performance. In this thesis, we will contribute knowledge in these areas. In Chapter~\ref{chapter4}, we use LTH to analyse weight distributions and language-specific subnetworks. In Chapter~\ref{chapter5}, we probe the layers of pretrained multilingual wav2vec 2.0 model. These models contain varying balances of mismatched pretraining languages. We assess how mismatched multilingual pretraining affects the findings on phonetic feature learning outlined in this section. Then finally in Chapter~\ref{Chapter6}, we contribute future approaches to improve multilingual pretraining.
